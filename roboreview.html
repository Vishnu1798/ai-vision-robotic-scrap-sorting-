Scrap Sorting System

Vision-Based Pick and Place Robot

A robotic system that automatically identifies scrap metal objects using a camera, calculates where they are in 3D space, and physically picks them up and drops them into a collection bin — all without any human involvement.

3-DOF Robotic Arm Computer Vision Analytic IK / FK PyBullet Simulation Raspberry Pi Hardware YOLOv8 Detection

Project Objectives

  • Automatically detect and identify scrap metal objects from non-scrap objects using a camera, without any human looking at the scene.
  • Find the exact 3D position (X, Y, Z coordinates in the real world) of each detected scrap object using camera depth and projection mathematics.
  • Calculate the correct joint angles for the robotic arm to reach each scrap object using a mathematical method called Inverse Kinematics.
  • Verify every planned arm movement using Forward Kinematics before the arm actually moves, to prevent unsafe or wrong motions.
  • Perform the complete pick-and-place sequence — hover, descend, grip, lift, transport to bin, and release — fully autonomously in a continuous loop.
  • Validate the entire system in a physics simulation before deploying on real hardware, to find and fix bugs safely.

Project Novelty

  • Integration of a full computer vision pipeline — camera capture, depth linearisation, pixel centroid detection, and colour classification — directly driving robotic arm movements with no manual coordinate input.
  • Use of the inverse View-Projection matrix to accurately convert 2D camera pixels into real 3D world coordinates, enabling the arm to reach objects at any position in its workspace.
  • Closed-form analytic Inverse Kinematics — the joint angles are computed mathematically in one step, with no numerical guessing or slow iteration, making the system fast and reliable.
  • Built-in Forward Kinematics verification as a safety check before every single move — if the math predicts the arm will land in the wrong place, the arm does not move at all.
  • Parallel simulation and hardware tracks sharing the same mathematics — every algorithm improvement tested safely in the virtual environment works identically on the physical robot.
  • The system is fully autonomous in a loop — it scans, picks, bins, and rescans without stopping until all scrap objects are removed from the work surface.

Degrees of Freedom

A Degree of Freedom (DOF) means one independent way the robot can move. Our arm has 3 DOF — three separate motors, each controlling one type of motion. Together, these three motions allow the gripper to reach any point in 3D space within the arm's reach.

🔄

DOF 1 — Yaw (Base Rotation)

The entire arm rotates left and right around a vertical axis, like a person turning to face a different direction. This points the arm toward the correct object. Joint angle: θ₁

⬆️

DOF 2 — Shoulder Pitch

The upper arm tilts up and down at the shoulder joint, like raising your arm. This controls how high the gripper reaches. Joint angle: θ₂

↩️

DOF 3 — Elbow Pitch

The forearm bends at the elbow, like bending your arm. This controls whether the arm is extended far out or folded close in. Joint angle: θ₃

Why 3 DOF is enough: Since all objects rest on a flat table at a known height, we only need to control X (left-right), Y (forward-back), and reach (extension). The yaw joint handles X-Y direction, while shoulder and elbow together control reach and height. A wrist and gripper are added as fixed/sliding joints for grasping, bringing total joints to 5, but the active positioning is driven by the 3 main DOF.

Inverse Kinematics

Inverse Kinematics (IK) answers this question: " what angles must each joint rotate to?" This is the opposite of Forward Kinematics

In this project, once the camera tells us where a scrap object is (its X, Y, Z coordinates), we use IK to calculate the three joint angles θ₁, θ₂, θ₃ needed for the gripper to reach that exact position. The method is analytic — meaning we derive exact mathematical formulas and compute the answer directly, without any slow guessing or iteration.

Step 1 — Yaw Angle (θ₁): Which direction to face?

Formula — Base Rotation
θ₁ = arctan2( Δy , Δx )
θ₁
The angle the base rotates. Points the arm in the direction of the object.
Δx
Horizontal distance (X) between object and arm base (in metres)
Δy
Lateral distance (Y) between object and arm base (in metres)
arctan2
A special version of the arctangent that works correctly in all four directions, unlike regular arctan which can give wrong answers for certain positions.

Step 2 — Reach Distance: How far is the wrist?

Formula — Horizontal and 3D Reach
r = √( Δx² + Δy² )
d = √( r² + Δz² )
r
Horizontal distance from arm base to the object on the table plane
Δz
Vertical distance from the shoulder joint to the wrist (height difference)
d
Straight-line distance from shoulder to wrist. If d is greater than L₂ + L₃ = 40 cm, the object is out of reach and the arm skips it safely.

Step 3 — Elbow Angle (θ₃): How bent is the arm?

Formula — Elbow Joint (Cosine Rule)
cos(θ₃) = ( d² − L₂² − L₃² ) / ( 2 × L₂ × L₃ )
θ₃ = arccos( cos(θ₃) )
θ₃
The elbow joint angle. Small = arm nearly straight. Large = arm deeply bent.
L₂
Upper arm length = 0.22 m (22 cm)
L₃
Forearm length = 0.18 m (18 cm)
d
Shoulder-to-wrist distance (from Step 2)
Cosine Rule
A triangle geometry rule: given all three side lengths, find any angle. Our triangle has sides L₂, L₃, and d — we find the elbow angle opposite side d.

Step 4 — Shoulder Angle (θ₂): How high to raise the arm?

Formula — Shoulder Joint
α = arctan2( Δz , r )
β = arctan2( L₃ × sin(θ₃) , L₂ + L₃ × cos(θ₃) )
θ₂ = α − β
α
The angle to raise the shoulder if the arm were one rigid rod pointing directly at the wrist
β
The correction needed because the arm bends at the elbow (the elbow affects where the shoulder must point)
θ₂ = α − β
Shoulder angle = direct elevation angle minus the elbow correction. The subtraction (not addition) is critical — using addition causes the arm to aim in the wrong direction.

Forward Kinematics Verification

After computing the three angles from IK, we immediately verify using Forward Kinematics (FK) — we plug θ₁, θ₂, θ₃ back into the FK equations and predict where the gripper tip would land. If the predicted position is more than 5 cm away from the intended target, the arm does not move. This prevents dangerous wrong movements before they happen.

Formula — Forward Kinematics Check
r_xy = L₂ × cos(θ₂) + L₃ × cos(θ₂ + θ₃)
r_z = L₂ × sin(θ₂) + L₃ × sin(θ₂ + θ₃)
X_tip = X_base + r_xy × cos(θ₁)
Y_tip = Y_base + r_xy × sin(θ₁)
Z_tip = Z_base + L₁ + r_z − L_G
r_xy
Total horizontal reach of the arm — upper arm horizontal + forearm horizontal
r_z
Total vertical rise of the arm tip above shoulder level
L_G
Gripper length (7 cm) — subtracted to get fingertip position from wrist position
FK Error
√((X_tip−X_target)² + (Y_tip−Y_target)² + (Z_tip−Z_target)²) — must be below 5 cm to proceed

System Workflow

The system runs as a continuous loop. After each successful pick, the camera rescans the table and the entire pipeline repeats until all scrap objects are gone. Here are all the steps in order:

Camera Scans the Table

The camera takes a photo of the table. It captures three things at once: an RGB colour image (what the scene looks like), a depth map (how far each pixel is from the camera), and a segmentation map (which object each pixel belongs to).

Find Each Object and Classify It

For each detected object, the system reads the colour at the centre pixel. If Red ≈ Green ≈ Blue (all three colour channels are nearly equal, meaning grey or metallic), the object is classified as SCRAP. If it has a strong colour (gold, red, blue), it is classified as NON-SCRAP and completely ignored.

Convert Pixel to 3D World Position

Using the camera's depth value at the object's centre pixel and the camera's mathematical projection model (its view and projection matrices), the system calculates the exact X, Y, Z position of the object in the real world — in metres from the arm's base.

Calculate Joint Angles (IK)

Using the 3D position, the Inverse Kinematics formulas compute the three joint angles needed for the arm to reach the object. If the object is outside the arm's reach (more than 40 cm away), it is skipped and the system moves to the next scrap object.

Verify with Forward Kinematics

The computed angles are fed back through FK equations to predict where the gripper tip would end up. If the predicted position is more than 5 cm from the target, the move is cancelled — no physical motion happens. This is the safety gate.

Arm Moves to Hover Position

The gripper opens wide. The arm moves to a position 6 cm directly above the scrap object — this is the hover position. Approaching from above prevents the arm from knocking the object sideways.

Arm Descends and Grips the Object

The arm descends to the object's height. The gripper closes, clamping the scrap object from both sides. A rigid constraint is created between the wrist and the object so it moves with the arm perfectly.

Lift and Transport to Bin

The arm lifts the object 25 cm above the table, clearing all obstacles. Then it moves to directly above the scrap collection bin. The gripper opens and the constraint is removed — the object drops into the bin.

Return Home and Repeat

The arm returns to its home (neutral) position. The camera scans again. If more scrap objects are still on the table, the whole loop repeats. The system stops only when no more reachable scrap objects are detected.

System Implementation

The implementation of this project involves two parallel systems that share the same core mathematics — a PyBullet physics simulation and a Raspberry Pi hardware robot. Both run the same vision pipeline and the same IK/FK equations.

Simulation Implementation (PyBullet)

The simulation is written entirely in Python. The robot arm is described using a URDF file (Unified Robot Description Format) — an XML file that specifies every link's shape, size, colour, and mass, and every joint's type, axis of rotation, and motion limits. PyBullet reads this file and creates a physically accurate 3D model of the arm.

PyBullet's virtual camera captures an RGB frame, a depth buffer, and a segmentation map every time the system needs to scan. The depth buffer stores non-linear values that are converted to true distances in metres using the linearisation formula. The segmentation map identifies which pixels belong to which object by their unique body IDs.

Grey cubes represent scrap metal. Gold spheres represent non-scrap items. Objects are spawned at random positions on the table within the arm's verified reachable zone. The physics engine runs for 400 time steps after spawning to let all objects settle naturally under gravity before sorting begins.

Hardware Implementation (Raspberry Pi)

On the physical robot, a USB camera provides live video frames via OpenCV. A custom-trained YOLOv8 neural network processes each frame in real time, detecting scrap objects and providing their bounding boxes and class labels. The centre of each bounding box gives the pixel centroid.

Since all objects rest on a flat table at a known height, the depth is fixed. The pixel centroid is converted to real-world X and Y coordinates using calibration scale factors (cm per pixel) measured with a physical ruler. This avoids the need for an expensive depth camera.

The Raspberry Pi sends angle commands to the PCA9685 PWM driver over I²C. The PCA9685 generates the correct PWM signals for each servo motor. Rather than jumping directly to the target angle (which would be violent and damaging), the system uses linear interpolation — it smoothly moves through 20–30 intermediate positions between the current angle and the target angle, with a small delay between each step.

The electromagnet is controlled by a 5v relay. When the arm descends to an object,the relay module activates the magnet and attracts the metallic scrap. When the arm is over the bin,the relay module activates the magnet and the object drops in.

Simulation Demonstration

Before building and testing on real hardware, the entire system was developed and validated inside a PyBullet physics simulation. This approach has several important advantages: it is completely safe (no physical damage possible), it runs faster than real time, the camera and physics are perfectly repeatable, and any bug in the kinematics or vision pipeline can be seen visually and fixed immediately.

The simulation environment contains a flat table, the 3-DOF robotic arm mounted at one end, 4 grey scrap cubes and 4 gold non-scrap spheres placed randomly on the table surface, and a brown collection bin placed within the arm's reach. A virtual overhead camera looks down at the table from [0.60, 0.00, 1.20] metres.

The simulation proves the full pipeline works end to end: the camera correctly identifies all four grey cubes as scrap, calculates their 3D positions accurately, computes valid IK angles for all of them, and picks and bins all four without ever touching a single gold sphere. All FK verification errors are exactly 0.000 m — confirming the IK mathematics are perfectly correct.

Servo Motor Control

The robot's joints are moved by servo motors — motors that can rotate to a precise angle (0° to 180°) and hold that position. They are controlled using Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) signals — a repeating electronic pulse where the width of the pulse tells the motor which angle to go to.

  • A typical servo motor expects a PWM signal that repeats every 20 milliseconds (50 Hz frequency). This is set using pca.frequency = 50 in the code.
  • A pulse width of 500 microseconds corresponds to 0° (full left). A pulse width of 2500 microseconds corresponds to 180° (full right). Values in between give intermediate angles.
  • The PCA9685 chip handles the precise PWM timing automatically. The Raspberry Pi just sends the target angle over I²C, and the PCA9685 generates the correct pulse width.
  • Smooth motion interpolation is used: instead of commanding the final angle at once, the code sends 20–30 intermediate angle commands with a 50 ms pause between each. This prevents the arm from jerking violently to its new position.
  • Each servo is assigned to one joint: Channel 0 = base yaw, Channel 1 = shoulder pitch, Channel 2 = elbow pitch. All three servos move simultaneously during each interpolation step.
  • Servo angles are clamped to the range [0°, 180°] in software before being sent, preventing the motor from being commanded to a physically impossible position.

Advantages

  • Fully autonomous: The system requires no human involvement during operation. Once started, it identifies, picks, and sorts all scrap objects on its own.
  • Mathematically exact: Analytic IK gives the exact correct answer every time — no approximation, no slow iteration, no possibility of the solver failing to converge.
  • Safe by design: The FK verification step acts as a mathematical safety net. Every planned move is checked before it happens. An incorrect calculation causes a skip, not a crash.
  • Simulation-validated: The complete system was tested and debugged in simulation before hardware deployment. This saves time, prevents damage, and gives full confidence in the code.
  • No depth camera needed on hardware: The calibrated flat-surface projection model gives accurate 3D positions without an expensive depth sensor, using only a standard USB camera.
  • Easily expandable: The pipeline is modular. Adding a depth camera, improving the detector, or using a longer arm only requires changing one module — not rewriting the whole system.

Applications

  • Recycling Plants: Automatically sort metallic scrap from non-metallic waste on a conveyor belt, reducing hazardous manual labour and increasing throughput.
  • Manufacturing Lines: Remove defective metal parts or scrap offcuts from a production line without stopping the process or needing a dedicated operator.
  • Warehouse Automation: Sort and place small metallic components into bins based on visual classification, enabling fully automated storage and retrieval.
  • Hazardous Environments: Operate in places unsafe for humans — high temperature, toxic fumes, or radioactive zones — where manual sorting is not possible.
  • Educational Robotics: A complete, working demonstration of computer vision + inverse kinematics + autonomous manipulation that students and researchers can learn from and build upon.
  • Pharmaceutical / Food Sorting: With different detection models, the same pipeline can sort capsules, bottles, or food items by colour or size on a packaging line.

Conclusion

This project successfully demonstrates a complete, working autonomous robotic sorting system. A 3-DOF robotic arm uses a camera to see scrap metal objects, calculates their exact 3D location in space using computer vision mathematics, computes precise joint angles using analytic Inverse Kinematics, verifies every move using Forward Kinematics, and then physically picks up and deposits each scrap object into a collection bin — all without any human input.

The two key technical achievements are: (1) a complete pixel-to-motion pipeline where every step from raw camera data to physical joint commands is mathematically grounded and implemented, and (2) correct analytic IK with a built-in FK safety check that ensures the arm only moves when the mathematics confirm the move is correct.

By developing and validating the system in PyBullet simulation first, all mathematical bugs were found and fixed safely before any risk to the hardware. The final result is a system that correctly removes all scrap objects and never touches any non-scrap object — proving the full pipeline from camera to bin works exactly as intended.
